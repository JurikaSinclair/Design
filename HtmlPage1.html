The Design Process

The design process is a systematic approach to problem-solving that designers use to create effective solutions. It involves several key stages:

1Identify the Problem

Clearly define what problem needs to be solved or what need must be addressed. This stage involves research to fully understand the context and constraints.

2Research & Investigation

Gather information about similar products, materials, technologies, and user needs. This research will inform your design decisions.

3Develop Design Criteria

Establish specific criteria that your design must meet. These criteria will serve as a guide throughout the design process and help evaluate your final solution.

4Generate Ideas

Brainstorm multiple possible solutions. Explore different approaches and concepts without judging them initially.

5Develop Solutions

Select the most promising ideas and develop them further. Create detailed drawings, models, or prototypes to explore how they might work.

6Testing & Evaluation

Test your solution against the design criteria. Identify strengths and weaknesses and consider how the design could be improved.

7Refinement

Make improvements based on testing and evaluation. This might involve several iterations to optimize your solution.

The design process is not strictly linear – designers often move back and forth between stages as they develop their solutions. For example, testing might reveal new information that requires revisiting the design criteria or generating new ideas.

Creating Effective Design Criteria

Design criteria are the specific requirements that your product must meet to be considered successful. Well-defined criteria provide clear direction for your design and serve as a reference point for evaluation.

PURPOSE

What is your product meant to do? What problem does it solve? What needs does it address?

FUNCTION

How will your product work? What actions must it perform? What are the essential operations it must complete?

TARGET MARKET

Who will use your product? What are their specific needs, preferences, and limitations?

FORM

What will your product look like? What size, shape, color, and aesthetics should it have?

Tips for Creating Effective Criteria:

  • Make your criteria short but as specific as possible
  • Avoid vague or general language
  • List primary criteria first, then secondary criteria
  • For clarity, display design criteria as bullet points
  • Ensure criteria are measurable whenever possible

Clock Project Example

Let's apply these principles to a clock design project. When designing a clock, consider the following:

Purpose
Function
Target Market
Form

Purpose Considerations for a Clock

  • Is the clock primarily decorative or functional?
  • Will it be used in a specific context (e.g., kitchen timer, alarm clock, wall clock)?
  • Does it need to serve additional purposes beyond telling time?

Example criteria:

  • The clock must be easily readable from a distance of 3 meters
  • The clock should blend with modern interior design
  • The clock could include a temperature display

Function Considerations for a Clock

  • How will the time be displayed (analog, digital, or both)?
  • What power source will it use?
  • What level of accuracy is required?
  • Will it need additional functions (alarm, timer, etc.)?

Example criteria:

  • The clock must maintain accurate time within ±1 minute per month
  • The clock should operate silently
  • The clock must run for at least 12 months on a standard AA battery

Target Market Considerations for a Clock

  • Who will be using the clock?
  • What environment will it be used in?
  • Are there any specific accessibility needs to consider?

Example criteria:

  • The clock must be suitable for elderly users with limited vision
  • The clock should be appropriate for a classroom environment
  • The clock must be affordable for the average household

Form Considerations for a Clock

  • What size and shape should the clock be?
  • What materials will be used?
  • What color scheme and aesthetic style is appropriate?

Example criteria:

  • The clock must be no larger than 30cm in diameter
  • The clock should use sustainable materials where possible
  • The clock must be waterproof for bathroom use

Sample Clock Design Criteria

Here's an example of a completed set of design criteria for a wall clock project:

MUST

  • Must display time accurately within ±30 seconds per week
  • Must be easily readable from across a classroom (6 meters)
  • Must be wall-mountable with secure fixings
  • Must operate for at least 1 year without battery replacement

SHOULD

  • Should have a modern design that fits in educational environments
  • Should have contrasting colors for easy readability
  • Should be silent during operation
  • Should be made from durable materials

COULD

  • Could include a second time zone display
  • Could incorporate sustainable materials
  • Could display the date

WON'T

  • Won't require internet connectivity
  • Won't cost more than $50 to produce
  • Won't make audible sounds (ticking or alarms)

Assessment Guidelines

When evaluating your design project, consider the following:

  • You should aim to have at least 5-8 well-defined criteria to achieve an A grade
  • Your criteria should be specific, measurable, and relevant to your design
  • Consider how well your final design meets each of your criteria
  • Evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of your design against the criteria
  • Suggest improvements based on your evaluation

Remember to add your design criteria to your project documentation and refer back to them throughout the design process!
